A silicon spin qubit is like the quantum-era cousin of the transistor. Instead of storing information using electrical charge (“0” or “1”), it encodes data in the **spin** of a single electron trapped in a **quantum dot**—a nanoscale potential well formed in silicon.

By applying precise voltages to nearby gate electrodes, an electron can be loaded into the dot. Its spin state can be flipped using microwave pulses, and neighbouring spins can be entangled to perform quantum logic. These qubits are fabricated using **standard CMOS processes**, offering a scalable path to millions of qubits—just like transistors enabled classical computing.

Silicon qubits benefit from:
- Low power consumption
- Long coherence times
- Compatibility with existing semiconductor infrastructure

---

### **Applications of Silicon Spin Qubits**

1. Quantum computing with dense qubit arrays
2. Quantum simulations using silicon quantum dots
3. Cryo-CMOS based on-chip quantum control electronics

---

### **Companies working in silicon spin qubits and their work / breakthrough**

1. **[Diraq](https://diraq.com/)**  
Sydney-based spin-out from UNSW. They are fabricating quantum-dot spin qubits using standard CMOS foundries to accelerate the development of million-qubit processors.

2. **[Quantum Motion](https://quantummotion.tech/)**  
London-based company building dense MOS silicon qubit arrays with cryo-CMOS control—enabling fault-tolerant architectures on classical wafers.

3. **[Silicon Quantum Computing](https://www.sqc.com.au/)**  
Led by Prof. Michelle Simmons. SQC uses atomic-scale placement of phosphorus donors in silicon to create ultra-coherent spin qubits.

4. **[Equal 1 Labs](https://www.equal1.com/)**  
Combines spin qubits and cryogenic control logic on a single chip. Their Q-SoC prototype enables rack-mount testing of spin-based quantum processors.

5. **[SemiQon](https://www.semiqon.tech/)**  
A Finnish fab-light venture developing low-cost, energy-efficient spin qubit chips for sustainable quantum data centres.

6. **[ARQUE Systems](https://www.arque.systems/)**  
Spin-off from Jülich and RWTH Aachen, developing a “qubit-shuttling” design to move electron spins on-chip for long-range interactions.

7. **[Intel Quantum](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/research/quantum-computing.html)**  
Developed a 12-qubit silicon spin chip called **Tunnel Falls**, distributed to global universities to accelerate collaborative quantum R&D.

8. **HRL Laboratories**  
Pioneered logic gates using encoded **three-spin qubits** in Si/SiGe quantum dots with high fidelities suitable for error correction thresholds.

9. **Infineon Technologies Quantum Group**  
Located in Dresden, exploring scalable fabrication of Si/SiGe spin qubits for integration into Europe’s quantum supply chain.

10. **[XeedQ](https://xeedq.com/)**  
Although primarily focused on diamond spin qubits, XeedQ has delivered **mobile multi-qubit processors** and explores hybrid spin-based platforms.

### **Universities working in silicon spin qubits**

1. **UNSW Sydney**  
Pioneered foundational work on quantum-dot and donor-based silicon qubits. Their spin-based research laid the groundwork for Diraq and SQC.

2. **University of Oxford**  
Part of **Quantum Motion**, contributing to the development of cryo-CMOS and silicon qubit scaling architectures.

3. **University of Cambridge**  
Conducts research on silicon-based quantum computing architectures and quantum-classical co-design in semiconductor devices.

4. **RWTH Aachen + Forschungszentrum Jülich**  
Collaborated to develop **ARQUE Systems**, focusing on moving spins across silicon chips to realise modular and scalable processors.

5. **TU Delft (QuTech)**  
Although better known for superconducting and NV center qubits, they’ve also contributed to foundational research on silicon quantum dots.
